Our Company's Information Security Policies

Incident Response: What to Do in Case of a Security Incident

A rapid and coordinated response is critical. Your immediate action can prevent a small incident from becoming a major crisis.

📘

Definition / Key Concept

What is a Security Incident?

Any event that compromises the Confidentiality, Integrity, or Availability (CIA) of our information systems or data.

Think: Anything that seems "off" or indicates something bad has happened to our data or systems.

Examples:

Your computer behaving unusually (slow, pop-ups, files disappearing).

You or a colleague accidentally clicked on a phishing email link or opened a suspicious attachment.

You notice unauthorized access to your account or files.

Your company laptop, mobile phone, or a USB drive containing company data is lost or stolen.

You observe suspicious network activity (e.g., unusually high internet traffic).

Accidental disclosure of confidential data (e.g., emailing sensitive information to the wrong external person).

You receive a message or screen demanding ransom to unlock your files/computer.

Importance of Prompt Reporting - The "Golden Hour"

Containment: The faster an incident is reported, the quicker our IT/Security team can investigate and contain the spread of the attack, limiting potential damage and impact. Every minute counts!

Damage Control: Reduces the overall impact on business operations, mitigates financial loss, and helps protect our reputation.

Legal & Regulatory Compliance: Enables us to meet mandatory reporting timelines (e.g., CERT-In's strict 6-hour window, DPDP Act breach notification requirements). Delaying reporting can lead to severe penalties.

Investigation: Allows our security team to collect crucial forensic evidence (logs, file timestamps) that helps them understand how the attack happened, who was responsible, and how to prevent future incidents.

What to Do if You Suspect an Incident - Your Action Plan

DO NOT Panic: Stay calm. A clear head helps you follow procedures.

IMMEDIATELY Disconnect (If Safe & Possible):

If you suspect malware, ransomware, or unauthorized access, disconnect your computer from the network. Unplug the Ethernet cable or turn off Wi-Fi.

Do not turn off or restart the computer unless specifically instructed by IT/Security (turning it off can destroy valuable forensic evidence needed for investigation).

DO NOT Try to Fix It Yourself: Do not attempt to investigate, delete files, run unauthorized tools, or try to "clean" your system. This can destroy critical evidence and worsen the situation.

REPORT IMMEDIATELY:

Contact the Designated Company Contact for Security Incidents (e.g., IT Helpdesk, Security Operations Center (SOC), or use the designated email/hotline).

Company Incident Response Hotline/Email: [Provide Specific Phone Number and/or Email Address - Make this prominent!]

Provide as much detail as possible:

What happened? (e.g., "I clicked a suspicious link," "My files are encrypted," "My computer is behaving strangely.")

When did it happen? (Date and time).

What did you observe? (Specific error messages, strange pop-ups, changed file names).

Any emails, messages, or files involved?

Your contact information.

Cooperate with Investigation: Be prepared to provide any information requested by the incident response team. They may need access to your device or accounts.

Do NOT Discuss Externally: Do not discuss the incident with unauthorized personnel, outside the incident response team, or on social media. Confidentiality is paramount during an incident.
