Conclusion & Q&A

Your Role & Responsibilities in Cybersecurity - BE OUR HUMAN FIREWALL!

Remember: Cybersecurity is a team sport. Your active participation is our strongest defense.

You are Our First Line of Defense!

No matter your role, your department, or your seniority, every single employee is personally responsible for safeguarding our company's information assets.

A strong security posture is a collective effort, not just an IT department's responsibility. You are the Human Firewall.

Your Key Responsibilities - Actionable Steps for Every Day

Be Aware & Educated:

Actively participate in all mandatory security training sessions like this one. Ask questions!

Stay informed about the latest cyber threats and trends. Read internal security newsletters or advisories.

Understand and follow all company security policies and procedures. Ignorance is not an excuse.

Protect Your Credentials (Your Digital Identity):

Use strong, unique passwords for all your company accounts. Aim for passphrases (e.g., MyCatLovesPizza!2025).

Enable and use Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) always, wherever it's available. It's your best defense against stolen passwords.

Never share your passwords with anyone, not even IT.

Never write down passwords in an unsecured location. Use a company-approved password manager if needed.

Exercise Caution Online (The Click Test!):

Be hyper-vigilant against phishing, spear-phishing, and all forms of social engineering attacks.

Before you click a link: Hover over it to see the actual URL. If it looks suspicious or redirects to an unfamiliar domain, DON'T CLICK.

Before you open an attachment: Verify the sender, especially if it's unexpected. If unsure, delete it or report it to IT.

Beware of urgency and unusual requests: Cybercriminals thrive on panic. Take a moment to think.

Verify the sender's identity: If an email seems off, call the sender on a known number (from the company directory, not the email signature) to confirm.

Secure Your Devices & Workspace:

Physical Security: Keep company-issued devices (laptops, phones) physically secure. Never leave them unattended in public places.

Lock Your Screen: Always lock your computer screen when you step away from your desk, even for a moment (Windows: Win + L, Mac: Ctrl + Cmd + Q).

Device Encryption: Ensure your device hard drives are encrypted. Check with IT if unsure.

Unauthorized Software: Do not install unauthorized software, games, or browser extensions on company devices. These can introduce vulnerabilities or malware.

Secure Disposal: Dispose of confidential physical documents by shredding them. Return company devices to IT for proper secure disposal.

Handle Data Responsibly:

Data Classification: Understand the classification levels of data you work with (e.g., Confidential, Restricted) and handle it accordingly (e.g., no confidential data on personal cloud drives).

Access Control: Only access data that is absolutely necessary for your specific job function. Don't snoop.

Secure Sharing: Use only company-approved secure channels for sharing sensitive data, both internally and externally. Avoid unsecured email or personal chat apps.

No Unauthorized Storage: Do not store sensitive company data on unauthorized personal devices, personal cloud services (like personal Google Drive/Dropbox), or external USB drives unless specifically approved and encrypted.

Encrypt Sensitive Files: When in doubt about sharing sensitive data, ask IT about encryption options.

Report Immediately (Don't Hesitate!):

Report any suspected security incident, suspicious activity (e.g., strange email, unusual computer behavior), or policy violation to [Designated Security/IT Contact/Hotline] without delay.

Report lost or stolen devices immediately. Time is critical for remote wiping or locking.

Your report is vital. It helps protect the entire organization. There will be no negative repercussions for reporting a suspected incident in good faith.

Think Before You Act:

Before any action, pause and consider the security implications.

If something feels "off" or too good to be true, it probably is a scam.

When in doubt, ask for help from IT/Security. We are here to support you!
