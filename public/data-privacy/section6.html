Introduction to the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (DPDP Act) - India

GDPR - Obligations for Data Controllers & Processors

Data Controllers and Processors must implement appropriate technical and organizational measures to ensure and demonstrate compliance.

Accountability Principle (Article 5(2) & 24):

Controllers are responsible for, and must be able to demonstrate compliance with, the GDPR principles. This isn't just about 'doing' privacy, but about 'proving' it.

Requires a proactive approach to compliance, beyond just having policies – it involves active monitoring, documentation, and continuous improvement.

Data Protection by Design and Default (Article 25):

By Design: Integrate data protection considerations into all processing activities from the outset of system design, product development, or process implementation. This means building privacy safeguards into the core architecture.

By Default: Ensure that, by default, only necessary personal data is collected, processed, and accessible. This means privacy settings should be at the highest level by default, requiring individuals to opt-in for broader sharing.

Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs) (Article 35):

Mandatory for processing operations likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of individuals.

A DPIA is a process designed to describe the processing, assess its necessity and proportionality, and help manage the risks to the rights and freedoms of data subjects.

Examples requiring DPIA: new technologies, large-scale processing of sensitive data, systematic monitoring of public areas, or processing that could lead to discrimination.

Data Protection Officer (DPO) (Article 37):

Mandatory appointment for certain organizations:

Public authorities.

Organizations whose core activities consist of large-scale, regular, and systematic monitoring of data subjects.

Organizations whose core activities consist of large-scale processing of special categories of data (sensitive data) or data relating to criminal convictions.

The DPO advises on GDPR compliance, monitors internal compliance, provides training, and acts as a contact point for supervisory authorities and data subjects.

Data Breach Notification (Articles 33 & 34):

To Supervisory Authority (DPA): Controller must notify the relevant Data Protection Authority (DPA) of a personal data breach without undue delay, and where feasible, not later than 72 hours after becoming aware of it, unless the breach is unlikely to result in a risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons.

To Data Subjects: If the breach is likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of individuals, the controller must also notify the affected data subjects without undue delay, providing clear information on the nature of the breach and mitigation steps.

Records of Processing Activities (RoPA) (Article 30):

Controllers and, in some cases, processors must maintain detailed records of all processing activities under their responsibility.

This includes purposes of processing, categories of data subjects, categories of personal data, recipients, international transfers, retention periods, and a general description of security measures.

International Data Transfers (Chapter V):

Transfer of personal data outside the EEA is generally restricted unless appropriate safeguards are in place to ensure the data retains a high level of protection.

Permissible transfer mechanisms include:

Adequacy Decision: European Commission has deemed the recipient country to have adequate data protection laws (e.g., Japan, South Korea).

Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs): Model clauses approved by the European Commission, which contractually bind the data importer to GDPR standards.

Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs): Internal rules for multinational corporations approved by DPAs for intra-group transfers, ensuring consistent protection across entities.

Derogations for specific situations (e.g., explicit consent, necessity for a contract).

Key Roles in Data Protection

Data Subject / Data Principal (Individual): The natural person to whom the personal data relates and who is identifiable. (e.g., our employees, customers, website visitors). They are the "owner" of the data.

Data Controller (GDPR) / Data Fiduciary (DPDP Act): The entity (e.g., our company) that, alone or jointly with others, determines the purposes and means of processing personal data. They are primarily responsible for ensuring compliance.
